# Product Principles

Based on Joshua Porter's *Principles of Product Design* (bokardo.com). The titles are Porter's; the commentary is condensed and rewritten for application. Use these when making product-level calls — what to build, what to cut, how to prioritize, how to position.

## 1. Usefulness is job #1

If no human finds use for it, the design has failed. Usefulness can be functional (it does a job), social (it signals something), or emotional (it means something). Any of those count — but one must be present.

## 2. The experience *is* the product

From the user's point of view there's no separation between the product and the experience of using it. Every surrounding interaction — signup, billing, support, offboarding — is part of what you're shipping. Don't polish the core and ignore the edges.

## 3. Solve existing problems

Product innovation is rarely inventing a new problem to solve. It's almost always solving a known problem meaningfully better than it's currently solved (Google Search, Netflix, Facebook). If you can't name the existing problem your product solves, be suspicious.

## 4. Look for investment

The strongest signal that a problem is worth solving is that people already spend money, time, or energy on it. Stated complaints are cheap; actual investment is expensive. Build where the investment already exists.

## 5. Model features on real artifacts

Watch for the hacks people use — post-it notes around a monitor, scotch tape over a sensor, a homegrown spreadsheet. Those artifacts are gold. They translate almost directly into useful features because someone already validated the demand by building the ugly version.

## 6. Fit and finish matter

Polish signals care. Care builds trust. A UI that's pixel-precise, copy that's clear, branding that looks professional — these tell users "the people behind this take it seriously, so I'll take it seriously too."

## 7. Release quality sets expectations

Every release trains users on what to expect next time. If 80% done becomes the norm, users learn to ignore releases. If every release — even a small one — is genuinely your best, people start paying attention.

## 8. Release a smaller, better product

Adding features has never been easier, but every feature is friction. The discipline is saying no. A focused product beats a feature-equal bloated one for the users you actually want.

## 9. The last 10% is the hardest

Good and great products share 90% of features. The last 10% — the details, the edge cases, the polish — is the differentiator, and it can take half the total time. That's not a bug in the process; it's the process.

## 10. Know who your real competitors are

Direct competitors are easy to see. Indirect competitors — email, Excel, a notebook, a group chat — are often more dangerous because they're already habituated. Map both before claiming a positioning.

## 11. Actual vs. desired use

There's usually a gap between how you want the product used and how it actually gets used. Be honest about the gap. Sometimes the actual use reveals a better product than the one you intended.

## 12. Personal value precedes social value

People rarely adopt software because "it's social." They adopt it because it gives them personal value, and social mechanics amplify from there. If your product requires a network to be useful on day one, you have a chicken-and-egg problem disguised as a feature.

## 13. Users are not product designers

Neil Gaiman's rule applies: when users say something is wrong, they're almost always right; when they tell you how to fix it, they're almost always wrong. Take complaints seriously; take proposed solutions as data about the underlying pain, not as specs.

## 14. The behavior you're seeing is the behavior you've designed for

If users do something unexpected, the design is producing it. That's not a moral claim — it's diagnostic. Accept the behavior as evidence and iterate on the design, not on the users.

## 15. Great products are focused on a single problem

The path to a broad market usually starts with being excellent for a narrow one. Trying to serve everyone from day one produces something that serves no one especially well.

## 16. Disruptive products look like toys

Disruptors often look unimpressive at launch — cheaper, simpler, less polished. That's the point. The incumbent underrates them until it's too late (Christensen's *Innovator's Dilemma*). If a competitor looks like a toy, pay attention.

## 17. Positioning is crucial

How you describe the product determines how users understand it and decide whether it's for them. Usually it's better to anchor on an existing category ("it's like X but for Y") than to claim a brand-new category — the latter requires teaching, which is expensive.

## 18. Product/market fit is when people sell for you

PMF isn't a metric with a clean threshold. It's when users spontaneously recruit other users, because the experience is good enough that recommending it serves *them*. When that happens, marketing becomes amplification rather than persuasion.

## Applying these in practice

When making a product call, sanity-check with these:

1. **Existing problem check** — What problem does this solve? Is it a real problem people already invest in?
2. **Single focus check** — Can we state the product's purpose in one sentence without "and" or "also"?
3. **Experience check** — What's the full experience, including signup, edge cases, and offboarding? Not just the hero flow.
4. **Artifact check** — What are users doing today, as a hack, to solve this? Can we model on that?
5. **Cut check** — Which three features, if removed, would make the product better?

If the answers are vague, the decision to build more likely isn't ready yet.
